Leverage AI to Redesign Your Job

16 copy-paste prompts that convert your Super Skills into measurable outcomes, eliminate low-value work, and position you for the roles AI can't replace.

How to Use These Prompts

Open our free Career GPT and paste any prompt below. Replace the [bracketed items] with your own information. Each prompt builds on the one before, so working through them in order gives you the most powerful results — but any single prompt works on its own.

The core idea: stop defining yourself by tasks you perform and start defining yourself by the outcomes you produce. That shift — from "Having" to "Doing" — is what makes you irreplaceable in the AI era.

Phase 1 — Discover Your Super Skills

Prompts 1–4
01
Uncover Your Super Skills
Identify the 3–5 strengths that set you apart — the skills where you consistently outperform.
›
I'm a [your job title] in [industry/company type]. My day-to-day work includes [list 5–7 key activities]. Help me identify my 3–5 "Super Skills" — the capabilities where I consistently deliver disproportionate value. For each one, explain why it's hard to replicate with AI and how it differentiates me from others in similar roles. Ask me clarifying questions if needed before finalizing the list.
Prompt
Why this matters: Most professionals can't articulate what they're best at. This prompt forces specificity and reveals the strengths you should build your career around — not the tasks you happen to do today.
02
Convert Super Skills into Performance Objectives
Transform abstract strengths into 6–8 measurable outcomes tied to business strategy.
›
My Super Skills are: [list from Prompt 1]. My company's top strategic priorities this year are [list 2–3 company goals or major projects]. For each Super Skill, write 1–2 specific performance objectives that tie directly to those strategic priorities. Format each as: "Use [Super Skill] to accomplish [measurable outcome] by [timeframe]." These should describe what I will deliver — not what I do day-to-day.
Prompt
Why this matters: This is the critical shift from "Having" to "Doing." Skills in a vacuum are just words on a resume. Skills tied to outcomes your company actually needs — that's a career move.
03
Build Your Achiever Pattern
Map the rising trajectory of your accomplishments to demonstrate growth potential.
›
Here are my last 3–4 roles or major projects in chronological order: 1. [Role/project, what you accomplished, approximate dates] 2. [Role/project, what you accomplished, approximate dates] 3. [Role/project, what you accomplished, approximate dates] Analyze the trend line. Is each role/project more complex, more ambiguous, and more demanding than the last? Identify the pattern of growth — what specific capabilities expanded from one to the next? Where is the trajectory pointing for my next move?
Prompt
Why this matters: The Achiever Pattern — a rising trajectory of accomplishment — is the strongest signal of high-potential talent. This prompt helps you see (and articulate) your own growth arc.
04
Stress-Test Your Value Proposition
Pressure-test whether your current positioning survives the next wave of AI capability.
›
I'm a [job title] whose Super Skills are [list] and whose key performance objectives are [list from Prompt 2]. Play devil's advocate. For each performance objective, rate on a 1–10 scale how likely AI is to be able to accomplish it independently within 2 years. For anything rated 7+, suggest how I should reframe the objective to emphasize the judgment, relationship, or strategic thinking component that AI can't replace. Be honest, not reassuring.
Prompt
Why this matters: The professionals who get disrupted are the ones who never asked this question. This prompt ensures your performance objectives are anchored to genuinely human capabilities.

Phase 2 — Audit & Filter Your Work

Prompts 5–9
05
The Pareto Role Audit
Identify which 20% of your activities produce 80% of your value.
›
Here's a list of everything I do in a typical week, with approximate hours spent on each: [List 10–15 activities with weekly hours, e.g., "Status update meetings — 4 hrs," "Building client proposals — 6 hrs," "Responding to internal Slack questions — 3 hrs"] Apply the Pareto Principle: which 20% of these activities produce roughly 80% of my measurable value? Which 30% consume the most time but contribute the least to outcomes? Present this as two lists with a clear gap analysis between where my time goes and where my value comes from.
Prompt
Why this matters: Most professionals spend 50%+ of their time on work that doesn't materially impact outcomes. You can't redesign your job until you see where the waste is.
06
The Covey Matrix Sort
Map your activities into Urgent/Important quadrants to find your strategic opportunity.
›
Using the same activity list from Prompt 5, sort each activity into Covey's four quadrants: • Quadrant I (Urgent + Important) — Crises, deadlines • Quadrant II (Important, Not Urgent) — Strategy, relationships, growth • Quadrant III (Urgent, Not Important) — Interruptions, most emails • Quadrant IV (Not Urgent, Not Important) — Busywork, time sinks Then tell me: What percentage of my week is spent in Quadrant II? That's the strategic work where high-impact careers are built. What specific Quadrant III activities should I automate or eliminate first to reclaim time for Quadrant II work?
Prompt
Why this matters: Quadrant II — important but not urgent — is where career-defining work happens: building relationships, developing strategy, learning new capabilities. Most people get trapped in Quadrants I and III.
07
Pull the Efficiency Lever
Use AI to automate your Quadrant III time-sinks and accelerate Quadrant I deadlines.
›
From my Covey Matrix analysis, my biggest Quadrant III time-sinks are: [list 3–4 activities], and my most pressured Quadrant I deadlines involve: [list 2–3 activities]. For each Quadrant III activity, give me a specific, step-by-step way to automate or radically simplify it using AI tools I can implement this week. For each Quadrant I activity, show me how AI can accelerate the execution so I finish faster. Include the specific AI tool or technique and a realistic time savings estimate.
Prompt
Why this matters: The Efficiency Lever is step one — doing the same job faster. But it's not the finish line. The time you reclaim here is the fuel for Prompt 8.
08
Pull the Redesign Lever
Stop doing the same job faster — ask whether the job itself should change.
›
I've reclaimed approximately [X hours/week] by automating low-value work. My Super Skills are [list] and my company's strategic priorities are [list]. Now pull the Redesign Lever: instead of doing the same job faster, how could I fundamentally redesign my role to invest this reclaimed time in higher-impact work? Suggest 3 specific ways I could expand my responsibilities upward — from execution toward strategy, cross-functional leadership, or solving problems one level above my current role. For each, describe the new performance objective and why it matters more to the company than what I was doing before.
Prompt
Why this matters: This is the critical pivot. Efficiency means doing someone else's job description faster. Redesign means rewriting your own job description around outcomes. The second is how careers are built.
09
The Outside-In Audit — Three Zoom Moves
See your work the way the system sees it — from the perspective of everyone who depends on your output.
›
I'm a [job title] in [function/department]. My primary outputs are [describe 2–3 key deliverables]. The functions that depend on my work include [list 2–3 downstream teams or stakeholders]. Run me through three "Zoom Moves" on my role: Zoom Out: Map how my work connects to the broader system. What breaks downstream if my output is late, wrong, or mediocre? What accelerates if it's exceptional? Show me where my real leverage is — it may not be where my job description says to focus. Zoom In: Pick the single highest-impact problem in my current work and do a structured root cause analysis. Push past the first plausible explanation to the structural cause. Zoom Forward: For any major initiative I'm working on, surface the unintended consequences. What incentives does my current process create that I didn't intend? Who is affected that I haven't considered? For each Zoom Move, give me one specific action I could take this week.
Prompt
Why this matters: Outside-in thinking is the principle that the people who create the most value aren't the ones who know every component in detail — they're the ones who understand what goes in, what comes out, and what happens downstream when something is off. This prompt trains that muscle on your actual work. From the article: Outside-In Thinking Is the Super Skill

Phase 3 — Position Yourself for What's Next

Prompts 10–12
10
Find Your Blind Spots
Uncover what you don't know you don't know — before it finds you.
›
I work in [role/function] at a [company type/industry]. My team's primary outputs are [describe what your team delivers]. Help me identify blind spots — things I might not know I don't know. Specifically: 1. What downstream effects does my work have that I probably never see (2–3 handoffs away)? 2. What assumptions does everyone in my function share that might be wrong? 3. What adjacent industry shifts could disrupt how my role works within 18 months? 4. What incentives does my current process create that I didn't intend? For each blind spot, suggest one question I could investigate this month to get ahead of it.
Prompt
Why this matters: The most dangerous risks aren't the ones you're managing — they're the ones you can't see. AI is uniquely good at surfacing second- and third-order effects that domain experts miss because they're too close to the work.
11
The Cross-Functional Value Map
Identify how your Super Skills create value for teams beyond your own.
›
My Super Skills are [list] and I currently sit in the [department/function]. The other functions I interact with regularly are [list 3–4 departments]. For each adjacent function, identify one high-value problem I could help solve using my Super Skills — something that would be a stretch assignment for me but would create measurable impact for that team. Frame each as a performance objective: "Use [Super Skill] to help [function] achieve [outcome]." Which of these represents the biggest career-growth opportunity for me?
Prompt
Why this matters: The professionals who advance fastest are the ones who solve problems across functions, not just within their lane. This prompt identifies where your skills can have outsized cross-functional impact.
12
Draft Your Redesigned Role Proposal
Create a concrete proposal to present to your manager for an expanded, outcome-based role.
›
Based on everything we've discussed — my Super Skills, performance objectives, Pareto audit, Covey analysis, and cross-functional opportunities — draft a one-page role proposal I can present to my manager. Structure it as: • Current state: what I spend most of my time on today • Proposed shift: what I would shift toward (with 4–6 new performance objectives) • How: specific AI workflows that free up time for higher-impact work • Impact: measurable value to the team and company strategy • Ask: what I need from my manager to make this transition (resources, access, authority) Keep the tone professional and confident, not apologetic. Frame this as a business case, not a personal request.
Prompt
Why this matters: Thinking about redesigning your job is step one. Actually proposing it — with a clear business case — is what separates people who talk about career growth from people who create it.

Phase 4 — Build the Habits

Prompts 13–16
13
The Weekly AI Experiment
Turn one real workflow into an AI-assisted process and measure the delta.
›
I want to run a one-week experiment. The workflow I'll test is: [describe a recurring task you do weekly — e.g., "preparing the Monday sales pipeline report"]. Currently it takes me approximately [X hours] and involves these steps: [list the steps]. Design an AI-assisted version of this workflow. For each step, tell me: what stays manual (and why), what AI handles, and what tool or prompt to use. Then give me a simple scorecard to track: time saved, quality difference, and anything I learned that I'd do differently next week.
Prompt
Why this matters: You don't learn AI by reading about it. You learn by running one real workflow through it this week and seeing what happens. Start small, measure the delta, iterate.
14
Become the AI Translator
Position yourself as the person who bridges AI capability and business context on your team.
›
My team of [size] in [function] has the following recurring pain points: [list 3–4 team-level frustrations, bottlenecks, or time sinks]. For each pain point, suggest one AI-powered improvement I could prototype and demo to the team within two weeks. For each, include: the specific tool or prompt, what it replaces, estimated time savings per person per week, and a one-sentence pitch I could use to get my manager's buy-in to pilot it. I want to become the person the team turns to when they ask "could AI help with this?"
Prompt
Why this matters: The most valuable people in the AI transition aren't the ones who understand AI best — they're the ones who understand the business well enough to know where AI should (and shouldn't) be applied.
15
Build Your Learning Network Sprint
Design a 30-day plan to connect with the people who will accelerate your AI learning curve.
›
I'm a [role] in [industry]. I want to build a small learning network of 5–8 people who are ahead of me in applying AI to work similar to mine. Help me design a 30-day sprint: • Week 1: Who should I look for? (What profiles, roles, or communities are most relevant?) • Week 2: How do I reach out? (Draft a brief, non-awkward message I can adapt) • Week 3: What should I ask in a first conversation? (5 questions that lead to actionable insight) • Week 4: How do I maintain the network without it feeling like work? I want a learning network, not just a contact list. The goal is people I can call when I'm stuck on a real problem.
Prompt
Why this matters: The data is clear — 70–85% of career opportunities come through networks, not job postings. And learning speed multiplies when you're connected to people solving similar problems.
16
The 90-Day Career Redesign Roadmap
Synthesize everything into a concrete, time-bound action plan you can actually execute.
›
Based on everything we've explored — my Super Skills ([list]), my performance objectives ([list top 4]), the time I can reclaim ([X hrs/week]), and the cross-functional opportunities I identified ([list 1–2]) — create a 90-day roadmap. Structure it as three phases: • Days 1–30: Quick wins — what I automate, what I stop doing, one experiment I run • Days 31–60: Visible impact — one cross-functional project I volunteer for, one redesigned workflow I demo • Days 61–90: Role shift — present my redesigned role proposal, measure results from Phase 1 and 2 For each phase, give me 2–3 specific actions with deadlines. Make it realistic for someone who still has a full-time job. I'd rather do 3 things well than 10 things poorly.
Prompt
Why this matters: Strategy without execution is a daydream. This prompt turns everything you've explored into a plan with dates, actions, and milestones. The professionals who redesign their careers are the ones who actually start.
