7 Overcoming Objections

▼
Key Principle: Don't give too much information away early. Stay in control by being vague initially, then gain candidate interest before revealing details. The key to effective recruiting is to get the person's attention, obtain a quick profile, and then either convince them to proceed or network if not qualified.

Universal Opening

"Would you be open to exploring a situation if it was clearly superior to what you're doing today?"

Never mention the title or location initially. Say: "I'm conducting a search for a senior-level position in [field], and your name came to my attention as someone I should connect with."

Common Objections & Responses

Objection 1: "Tell me about the job first"
"Please tell me a little about the job first, to see if I'm interested."

Don't give details yet. Be vague and reiterate:

"I'm conducting a very confidential senior-level search. I'll be happy to give as much information as you need, but I'd first like to just obtain a two-minute overview of your background."

You can add: "I have a couple of different spots, so I need to know which one you're most qualified for" OR "I'd like consider you for future opportunities if this one isn't big enough or in the wrong area."

Objection 2: "I'm too busy"
"I'm too busy to talk."

Say "fine" and ask for just 4 minutes - 2 for their overview, 2 to explain the job. Everyone will give you 4 minutes.

This is normally an "I'm not interested" objection disguised, so persist. Get their profile quickly, then describe the job in compelling fashion. Express slight concern that they might not be strong enough in a certain area (create job stretch) to build interest.

Critical: Get their home number. If you can't, you're not doing your job as a recruiter.

Objection 3: "Already interviewing elsewhere"
"I'm already well down the path with a few other companies."

This is a great sign - they're strong candidates if others want them too. Don't give up!

"That's fine, but let me ask you this: If the position I'm representing is better than what you're now considering, wouldn't it make sense to at least compare the two?"

Get their profile, ask about the other opportunities and comp. Position your role as having more stretch by describing what they'll learn, do, and become (use the performance profile).

Objection 4: "Happy where I am"
"I like my job, and I don't want to consider anything else right now."

Two approaches:

1. Delayed frontal attack: "That's great, but 95% of the people I call are at least open to exploring a situation. What about your job is so compelling that you wouldn't even consider evaluating something else?"

During their response, interject: "Your background is great; I'd certainly like to consider you for this job, or perhaps something in the future. Would you be open to this?"

2. End-around approach: Use networking as the entry point. "Since you're not interested, let me tell you about it anyway - someone you know might find this right up their alley." Give your 2-minute compelling job profile, then either network or ask if they'd reconsider.

Objection 5: "Won't relocate"
"I don't want to relocate."

Don't try to overcome this in the first call. There are at least three steps to getting someone to consider relocation - this is a process, not a single conversation.

For now, acknowledge it and focus on networking or future opportunities. Build the relationship first.

Close Upon an Objection Technique

Use this to uncover real concerns:

"I can certainly understand why that would be of concern. If we could address this issue satisfactorily, which I think we can, would this then be a position of interest for you?"

This helps you identify if the objection is real or just a smokescreen for other concerns.

Setting Up the Interview

Goal: 100% of the best are willing to proceed. Use this multi-step approach:

Interview Setup Process

Multi-Step Testing of Interest

  1. End of First Interview: Create supply and demand, test interest. Find out why candidate wants to move forward or not.
  2. Test at Each Event: Get commitment at each level (e.g., 2nd interview callback)
  3. Preliminary Offer Test: "If we could put an offer together in the range we discussed, what are your thoughts?"
  4. Secondary Close: "If we could meet your needs on all these areas, when would you be in a position to start?"
  5. Close Upon an Objection: "If we agree on that issue, when would you be in a position to accept?"
  6. Final Offer: "If I can get a formal offer on these terms, when can you give me official acceptance?"

Note: If longer than 24 hours for acceptance, back up and continue testing for objections.

Balance Sheet Close Technique

Use this powerful closing technique when candidates are on the fence:

  1. Draw two columns: Pros and Cons
  2. Work together with candidate to list all the pros
  3. Let the candidate develop all the cons alone
  4. Compare lists side by side
  5. Use opportunity gap, growth potential, and stretch to offset any compensation concerns

Key phrase: "The best performers want opportunity more than immediate compensation."

8 Closing Win-Win Offers

▼

The 5 Rules

The 1-10 Test

"On a scale of 1-10, where does this rank vs other options?"

  • 9-10: Ready to close
  • 7-8: "What would make it a 10?"
  • Below 7: Probe deeper

PBH Offer Testing Checklist

Follow this 8-step sequence to test and close offers systematically:

Step 1: Confirm Career Move First

Expected: Candidate affirms role is superior based on stretch, impact, growth, or satisfaction.

Step 2: Compensation Range Probe

Expected: Candidate confirms career move outweighs initial comp concerns.

Step 3: Unsigned Offer Sheet (Optional)

Expected: Uncovers hidden dealbreakers early.

Step 4: The 1–10 Test of Interest

Expected: If <9, recruiter identifies gaps and addresses them.

Step 5: Close Upon a Concern

Expected: Confirms if concern is real or a smokescreen.

Step 6: Trial Close with Start Date

Expected: Candidate gives a clear start date = high intent.

Step 7: Final Close

Expected: Verbal acceptance before formal offer letter is drafted.

Step 8: Buyer's Remorse Guardrail

Expected: Surface concerns before official offer to reduce risk of counteroffers.
