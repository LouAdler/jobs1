Performance-based Hiring

This wizard converts a traditional skills-and-experience job description into a performance-based job description — one that defines the 6–8 key performance objectives (KPOs) a person needs to accomplish to be considered successful. This is the foundation of Performance-based Hiring.

Work through each step with the hiring manager. Answer the questions in the middle column to fully understand the role. Use the right column to draft each KPO using the "Action Verb – Task – Result (KPI or deliverable) – Timeframe" format. Then select the top 6–8 for the final job description. During interviews, you'll ask candidates for accomplishments most comparable to these KPOs.

Steps Questions to Define the Job Action-Task-Result-Time
Start by Defining the Job, Not the Person Doing the Job!
Identify the top 2–3 job responsibilities and answer the questions in the next column to better understand how the new hire needs to handle these responsibilities.
Given the responsibilities of the job, describe what the person needs to do to ensure they're handled properly?
What are some of the projects and tasks the person will be working on in this job as part of these responsibilities?
Given the responsibilities of the job what do the best people in the role do differently or better than an average person?
Convert Skills, Experiences and Competencies into Outcomes
Identify the top 4–5 "must have" skills, competencies, and experience requirements. Then answer the questions in the second column for each one.
How will (trait, skill or experience) actually be used on this job?
Doing the same work, what do the best people do differently than an average person?
How long would it normally take for a top person to learn how to handle this successfully?
Determine the Major Objectives
For management roles with lots of change it's best to start with this step. Most jobs have 2–3 major objectives during the first year. Bigger jobs have longer time spans.
What's the biggest task(s) or project the person needs to finish over the course of the first year?
Consider:
  • The biggest technical and/or team challenge
  • Major changes, problems or improvements
  • Processes that need to be redesigned or upgraded
  • Big projects the person will lead
For process or staff roles
How long will it take the person to get up to speed in this role? Given this, what's the difference between exceptional and average performance?
Understand Team Issues
Draw a 360° work chart showing who the person will be working with on a regular basis inside and outside the company. Identify where team skills are essential for job success.
Who does the person work with on a regular basis?
From a team, EQ or interpersonal basis what is the difference between how the best people work with others vs. average and below average?
Are there any specific team challenges the person will need to handle in this role?

Use Timeline to Develop the "Process of Success"
Identify the critical subtasks that directly affect or impact achieving the major objective.
What does the person need to do in the first 30, 60, 90 days to ensure the major KPOs are met?
What would a top person do first?
Problem-solving and Thinking Skills
What types of problems will this person typically face in this role? These could be technical, team or business related.
Describe the Environment
The environment includes the pace, the decision-making process, the company culture and the hiring manager's style.
What's the culture like? Political?
Is the process for making decisions formal or informal?
Is it more entrepreneurial or bureaucratic?
Are there any aspects of the job that could negatively impact the person's performance?
Consider a difficult hiring manager, the intensity of the pace, some odd culture issues, old tech, tight deadlines, limited budget, lack of resources or challenging business issues.
Develop the Employee Value Proposition (EVP)
The EVP describes why the job could be a worthwhile career move for the candidate.
Why would a top person with multiple opportunities want this job for something other than the money?

KPO Examples by Category

Starter Kit Edition — Reference Guide
Performance-based Hiring
Use these examples to help write KPOs for your performance-based job description. Each KPO should clarify the task, define a successful result with a metric or deliverable, and include a timeframe.
Major Objectives — Projects
Collaborate with engineering, operations and sales to develop the three-year product roadmap. Get executive approval and budget within 10 months.
Major Objectives — Process
Within six months achieve quota 90% of the time.
Within 3–4 months develop 10–20 high quality leads per month and arrange 50% onsite visits with the sales team.
Outcomes — Convert "Having" to "Doing"
Communication skills → Present weekly progress reports to the executive team on the status of the marketing launch program.
Technical expertise → Use in-depth experience in (skill) to develop final design within 90 days.
Influencing others → Get the cross-functional product team to agree to the specs in order to meet challenging delivery dates.
"Results oriented" → Proactively deal with problems in the (area) and work with team to develop immediate solutions.
30-60-90 Day Subtasks
During the first 30 days, conduct an in-depth process review and identify any critical issues.
Assess the team's ability to achieve the objectives.
Prepare a detailed plan with contingencies.
Identify critical bottlenecks and develop workarounds.
Team, EQ, and Interpersonal KPOs
Meet with the staff and assess their ability to handle the revised plan.
Lead a cross-functional team consisting of (describe) to upgrade the (joint project) by (timeframe).
Manage a dysfunctional team of creative designers.
Reorganize the group based on the company's new direction.
Problem-solving and Critical Thinking
Evaluate the current approach to determine viability.
Develop workaround for a critical design flaw.
Implement a new system with limited resources.
Environment, Culture, and Hiring Manager
Complete essential KPOs given extremely tight deadlines.
There are limited resources with a need to make instant decisions with incomplete information.
The pace is intense with the expectation that the work will get done with "no excuses."
EVP and Job Branding
Use your (critical skill) to architect a new system.
Lead the entire marketing effort to launch a critical new product line.
Become an expert in (field) as part of our (education program).
