Two Hiring Mistakes.
One Is Easy to Fix.

Every hiring decision lands in one of four outcomes. Understanding the difference between a Type I and Type II error is the first step toward building a hiring process that actually works.

The Framework

Borrowed from Statistics. Built for Hiring.

In statistical theory, a Type I error is a false positive — you concluded something was true when it wasn't. A Type II error is a false negative — you missed something real. Applied to hiring, these two error types define the entire landscape of hiring failure.

"Most organizations obsess over avoiding Type I errors and end up committing Type II errors by the hundreds — never even knowing it."

Type I Error · False Positive

Hiring the Wrong Person

You offered the job to someone who turned out to be a poor performer. The cost is visible: low output, culture drag, remediation time, and eventual termination.

■ Addressable · Single failure point
Type II Error · False Negative

Losing a Top Performer

A top person was available but you never hired them. Maybe you never found them. Maybe your process repelled them. Maybe you lost them at the offer. The cost is invisible — and compounding.

▲ Systemic · Three failure points
Why Type II Is Different

Three Ways to Lose a Top Person — Any One Is Fatal

Stopping a Type I error requires better judgment at a single moment: the hiring decision. Stopping Type II errors requires a complete system — because the top candidate can be lost at any of three entirely different stages, and each loss is permanent.

1

Never seen — Sourcing gap

The top person isn't passive-job-board visible. Your job description is credential-based, not performance-based, so the search returns the wrong pool. The best candidate never enters your funnel at all.

2

Seen but not engaged — Process gap

You found them, but your process signaled mediocrity. A slow response, a generic req, a recruiter who led with requirements instead of opportunity. Top people interview you as much as you interview them — and they walked.

3

Engaged but not won — Offer and close gap

They liked what they saw, but you lost the close. A below-market offer, a slow decision, a competing offer you never anticipated. No one tested their interest before the formal offer. They accepted elsewhere.

⚠ A top performer lost at any stage is lost forever. This is why Type II errors require a complete hiring system — not a single fix.

The Asymmetry

Why Type II Demands a Complete Hiring System

Stopping Type I errors is a judgment improvement: use structured interviews, define performance outcomes before you meet candidates, score on evidence rather than gut feel. It's hard to do consistently, but it's a bounded problem with known interventions.

Stopping Type II errors is categorically different. Because a top candidate can be lost at three completely independent stages — sourcing, engagement, or closing — no single tool or training session fixes it. Every stage must work, in sequence, every time.

01

Find them

Performance-based job descriptions cast a different net. Proactive sourcing reaches passive candidates who never see your postings.

02

Engage them

Top candidates evaluate your process in real time. Speed, clarity, and a compelling opportunity narrative determine whether they stay in.

03

Win them

Testing interest before extending the offer, understanding competing priorities, and crafting a Win-Win offer closes the loop.
